Pregnancy and dental treatment: what is safe?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If you are pregnant or planning to be, you may wonder what dental treatment is safe and what should wait. It is a sensible question, and the guidance is fairly clear: some care is important during pregnancy, while elective treatment is usually best postponed. This article explains the general picture. For your own situation, always follow the advice of your dentist and doctor, who know your circumstances.

A note before we start

This article gives general information, not personal medical advice. Pregnancy is individual, and decisions about treatment should always be made with your own dentist and doctor, who can weigh your specific situation. What follows is the broad picture to help you understand the considerations, not a substitute for professional guidance tailored to you.

The general rule

Keep up essential dental care during pregnancy. Postpone elective cosmetic treatment until after. Always follow your own dentist and doctor.

Why dental health matters in pregnancy

Looking after your teeth and gums during pregnancy is important. Hormonal changes can make gums more prone to inflammation, sometimes called pregnancy gingivitis, so good cleaning and check-ups matter. Keeping your mouth healthy is part of looking after yourself during pregnancy, and routine care like check-ups and cleaning is generally considered fine, though your dentist will always take your pregnancy into account in what they do and when.

What is usually best postponed

Elective treatment, especially cosmetic work like veneers or a smile makeover, and non-urgent procedures, are usually best postponed until after pregnancy. There is no need to take on elective treatment during this time, and waiting removes any unnecessary consideration. This is particularly relevant to planning a treatment trip abroad: a cosmetic dental holiday is sensibly delayed until after pregnancy, when you can approach it freely and enjoy it.

Urgent problems should not be ignored

On the other hand, a dental problem causing pain or infection should not simply be ignored because you are pregnant, since an untreated infection carries its own risks. Necessary treatment for a genuine problem can usually be provided safely during pregnancy, with your dentist and doctor taking appropriate care over timing and approach. The message is not "avoid all treatment", but rather "keep up essential care, postpone the elective, and always be guided by your professionals".

Looking after your teeth during pregnancy

Since good oral health matters more during pregnancy, a few simple habits help you through it. Brush twice a day and clean between your teeth, paying attention to the gum line, since hormonal changes can make gums more prone to bleeding and inflammation. If morning sickness is an issue, the stomach acid from being sick can affect tooth enamel, so it helps to rinse your mouth with water afterwards and wait a little before brushing rather than brushing straight away, which can wear softened enamel. Keep up your regular dental check-ups and tell your dentist you are pregnant, so any care is given with that in mind. Cravings for sugary foods are common, and while there is no need to be hard on yourself, being aware that frequent sugar raises the risk of decay lets you take simple steps like rinsing or choosing less sugary options where you can. None of this is about adding pressure at an already busy time. It is about small, easy habits that protect your teeth and gums through the hormonal changes of pregnancy, so you come out the other side with your oral health intact and can then plan any elective treatment freely.

Planning treatment around pregnancy

If you are planning elective dental work, including a treatment trip, and are pregnant or trying to conceive, the simple approach is to plan it for after pregnancy. This keeps things straightforward and lets you have treatment when you can approach it without added considerations. If you have an urgent dental problem now, see a dentist promptly and tell them you are pregnant so they can care for you appropriately. Above all, let your own dentist and doctor guide your decisions, since they know your situation. Our candidate article covers wider planning.
