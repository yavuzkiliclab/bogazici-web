Bone grafting and sinus lift: rebuilding for implants

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If a dentist has told you that you need a bone graft or a sinus lift before implants, it can sound alarming. It is not. These are routine, well-established procedures that rebuild the foundation an implant needs, and they are the reason a lack of bone rarely rules implants out. This article explains what they are, why they are needed, and what to expect.

Why bone matters for implants

An implant anchors into the jawbone, so it needs enough healthy bone to hold it. The problem is that bone shrinks after a tooth is lost. Without a root stimulating it, the bone in that spot gradually reduces, sometimes over years before you even consider implants. Where there is not enough left to anchor an implant securely, the bone can often be rebuilt first. That is what grafting and sinus lifts do.

The key point

Not enough bone is usually a step to take first, not a reason you cannot have implants.

What a bone graft is

A bone graft adds material to an area where bone is lacking, giving the body a scaffold to build new bone around. Over a healing period, your own bone grows into and replaces the graft, creating a solid foundation for an implant. The material used can come from several sources, and your dentist will explain which suits your case. It is a common, predictable procedure, not an exotic one.

What a sinus lift is

A sinus lift is a specific kind of graft used in the upper back jaw, where the sinus cavity sits close above the bone. When there is not enough height there for an implant, the sinus membrane is gently lifted and bone is added beneath it, creating the height needed. It sounds dramatic but is a well-established technique done routinely. Once healed, it provides the bone an implant needs in an area that otherwise often lacks it.

What to expect

These procedures are done under local anaesthesia, with sedation available for anxious patients. There is a healing period afterwards, during which the new bone matures before an implant is placed. This is why a case needing grafting takes longer overall: the foundation has to be built and given time to set. Sometimes a small graft can be done at the same time as implant placement; a larger one is staged first. Your dentist plans the sequence for your case. You can read how implants are then placed in our implant guide.

Recovery after grafting

Recovery from a graft or sinus lift is usually more straightforward than people expect. Mild swelling and tenderness for a few days is normal, managed with prescribed medication and soft food, much like any minor oral procedure. The bigger part is patience rather than discomfort: the new bone needs months to mature before it can hold an implant, so the main thing asked of you is time. During that period you carry on normally. Following the aftercare instructions closely in the first days, and not smoking, gives the graft the best chance to take well. Your dentist checks progress before moving to implant placement, so nothing is rushed. Our warning signs article helps you tell normal recovery from anything worth flagging.

Why this is good news, not bad

Being told you need a graft is easy to hear as a setback. It is better understood as the opposite. It means the clinic is building a proper foundation so your implant lasts, rather than placing one into insufficient bone and hoping. A clinic willing to do the groundwork is a clinic taking the long view of your result. If you were told elsewhere that you did not have enough bone for implants at all, it may be worth a second opinion, since grafting often makes them possible.
