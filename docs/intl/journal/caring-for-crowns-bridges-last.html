Caring for crowns and bridges: making them last

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

A crown or bridge is an investment, and how you care for it decides how long it lasts. The good news is that the care is simple and mostly the same as looking after natural teeth, with a couple of specific points for bridges. This article covers how to keep crowns and bridges healthy for years.

The foundation: they still need cleaning

A crown or bridge covers a real tooth, and that tooth and its gum still need care. Decay can still form at the edge where a crown meets the tooth, and gum disease can still affect the support beneath. So the basics matter: brush twice a day, clean between your teeth daily, and keep regular check-ups. Looking after the tooth and gum under a restoration is looking after the restoration itself. Neglect, not normal wear, is what shortens their life.

The simple rule

Clean the edges and the gum line well. That is where crowns and bridges are lost, not from wear.

Cleaning around a crown

A crown is cleaned like a natural tooth, with particular attention to the gum line where crown meets tooth, since this margin is where decay can start if plaque builds up. Regular brushing and cleaning between the teeth keeps this area healthy. Your dentist checks the margins at check-ups, catching any issue early. Good cleaning here is the single most important habit for a long-lasting crown.

Cleaning under a bridge

A bridge needs one extra step: cleaning underneath the false tooth that spans the gap. Because this section sits above the gum with a space beneath, food and plaque can gather there, threatening the supporting teeth. Special floss threaders or small interdental brushes let you clean under the bridge, and your dentist shows you how. This is the key habit for a bridge, protecting the very teeth that hold it in place, as our bridges article explains.

Habits that protect them

A few sensible habits extend the life of any restoration. Avoid using your teeth as tools or biting very hard objects, which can damage crowns as they can natural teeth. If you grind your teeth, a night guard protects your restorations from cracking. Be a little careful with very sticky or very hard foods on a bridge. None of this is restrictive, it is simply treating your dental work with the same sense you would apply to your natural teeth.

Signs something needs attention

Part of caring for a crown or bridge is knowing when to get it checked rather than waiting. A few signs are worth acting on. If your bite suddenly feels different or the restoration feels high, a small adjustment usually sorts it, but leave it and it can stress the tooth. If you notice sensitivity to hot or cold around a crowned tooth, or an ache when you bite, that can signal decay at the margin or an issue with the tooth beneath, and it deserves a look. A bad taste or persistent bad breath around a bridge can mean plaque is gathering underneath where cleaning is not reaching. And if a crown or bridge feels loose in any way, see a dentist promptly rather than chewing on it, since a loose restoration can let bacteria in and damage the tooth. None of these means disaster, and caught early most are simple fixes. The point is that a restoration is not fit-and-forget: paying attention to these signs is what turns a potential problem into a quick, minor repair.

Regular check-ups matter

Professional check-ups are part of caring for crowns and bridges, not an optional extra. Your dentist checks the fit, the margins, the supporting teeth and the surrounding gum, catching small issues before they become big ones. Professional cleaning keeps the gum line healthy. For patients treated abroad, our remote follow-up and UK partner network mean this oversight does not stop when you fly home. With good daily care and regular checks, quality crowns and bridges last many years.
