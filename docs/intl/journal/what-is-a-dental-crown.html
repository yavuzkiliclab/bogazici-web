What is a dental crown, and when do you need one?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

A dental crown is one of the most common restorations in dentistry, but a lot of patients are unsure what it actually is or when they really need one. Understanding it helps you judge whether a crown is the right answer for your tooth, or whether something more conservative would do. This article explains what a crown is, when it is genuinely needed, and what to ask.

What a crown actually is

A crown is a cap that covers a whole tooth, restoring its shape, strength and appearance. Think of it as a custom-made cover fitted over what remains of a tooth, protecting it and letting it work normally again. Fitting one involves reducing the tooth to a smaller core, then bonding the crown over it. Because it covers the entire tooth, a crown restores a tooth that is too damaged for a simple filling but still has a sound root to build on.

In short

A crown caps a whole damaged tooth to restore it. It is a repair for a tooth that needs it, not a routine cosmetic choice.

When you genuinely need a crown

Crowns are the right answer in specific situations: a tooth broken or badly decayed beyond what a filling can restore, a tooth weakened after root canal treatment, a cracked tooth needing protection, or a large old filling that has failed. In these cases, a crown does something a filling cannot, holding a compromised tooth together and letting it function. This is restorative dentistry doing its core job: saving and strengthening a tooth that would otherwise be lost.

When a crown may be too much

Because a crown removes a lot of tooth, it should not be the default for small problems or purely cosmetic wishes. A small cavity needs a filling, not a crown. A cosmetic change on a healthy tooth is usually better served by a veneer, which removes far less, as our veneers vs crowns article explains. If a crown is proposed for a healthy tooth for looks alone, it is fair to ask whether a more conservative option would achieve the same goal.

What to ask about a proposed crown

If a dentist recommends a crown, a few questions clarify whether it is right. Why does this tooth need a crown rather than a filling or veneer? How much tooth will be removed? What material do you recommend and why? A good dentist explains the reasoning clearly and chooses a crown because the tooth needs it, not as a routine upsell. Our crown materials article covers the options, and our treatment guide the wider picture.

How long a crown lasts

A well-made, well-fitted crown on a healthy foundation can last many years with good care. Its lifespan depends on the material, the skill of the fitting, the health of the tooth and gum beneath, and how well you look after it. Good daily cleaning and regular check-ups protect both the crown and the tooth under it. Like any restoration it may eventually need replacing, but a quality crown is a long-lasting solution.
