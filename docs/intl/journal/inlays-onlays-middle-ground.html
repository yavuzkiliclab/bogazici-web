Inlays and onlays: the middle ground between filling and crown

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Between a simple filling and a full crown sits a lesser-known middle ground: inlays and onlays. For the right tooth, they can restore damage while preserving more of your natural tooth than a crown would. They are worth understanding, because a good dentist reaches for them when they are the conservative right answer. This article explains what they are and when they make sense.

The middle ground explained

An inlay or onlay is a custom-made restoration, crafted in a laboratory and bonded onto a tooth, that repairs damage too large for a simple filling but not extensive enough to need a full crown. An inlay fits within the cusps of a tooth; an onlay covers one or more cusps as well. They sit precisely between a filling and a crown in how much tooth they replace, and crucially, in how much they preserve.

Why they matter

Inlays and onlays fix bigger damage while keeping more natural tooth than a crown. Conservative dentistry at its best.

When they are the right choice

Inlays and onlays suit a tooth with damage or a failed large filling that is too much for a new filling but where a full crown would remove more healthy tooth than necessary. In these cases they offer the best of both: a strong, durable, well-fitting restoration that keeps more of your natural tooth intact. For a tooth that falls in this middle zone, they are often the most conservative sound option, which is exactly what good dentistry aims for.

How they compare with a crown

The key advantage over a crown is preservation. A crown covers the whole tooth and removes a lot of structure; an inlay or onlay repairs only the damaged part, keeping more healthy tooth. Both are strong and long-lasting when well made. So where a tooth does not truly need the full coverage of a crown, an inlay or onlay achieves a durable repair with less loss. This is the same conservative principle behind favouring veneers over crowns for cosmetic cases, as our veneers vs crowns article discusses.

How they compare with a filling

Compared with a large direct filling, inlays and onlays are stronger and often longer-lasting for bigger repairs, because they are precisely made in a laboratory and bonded to fit. A very large filling can weaken a tooth or fail over time, whereas an inlay or onlay restores strength more reliably. For a moderate-to-large repair, they can be the more durable choice, which is why a dentist may recommend one over simply placing a big filling.

Why they are worth asking about

Inlays and onlays matter because they represent conservative dentistry: fixing a tooth properly while removing as little healthy structure as possible. If you are told a tooth needs a crown, it is reasonable to ask whether an inlay or onlay would do the job with less tooth removed, where appropriate. A dentist who considers them is one thinking about preserving your teeth, not defaulting to the most extensive option. Our what is a crown article covers when full coverage is genuinely needed.
