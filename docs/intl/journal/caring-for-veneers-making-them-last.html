Caring for veneers: making them last

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Veneers are an investment in your smile, and how you look after them decides how long they stay looking their best. The good news is that caring for veneers is not complicated. It is mostly the same habits that keep natural teeth healthy, with a few specific do's and don'ts. This article covers how to make your veneers last.

The foundation: treat them like teeth

Veneers are bonded to real teeth, and those teeth and gums still need looking after. Brush twice a day, clean between your teeth daily, and keep your regular check-ups. The gum line where a veneer meets the tooth is where problems can start, so cleaning there matters. Good daily care protects both the veneers and the natural teeth underneath them. Neglect is what shortens their life, far more than normal wear.

The simple rule

Treat veneers like good teeth, not tools. Clean them well, and do not ask them to do a bottle opener's job.

What to avoid

  • Using teeth as tools: do not bite nails, open packaging or crunch ice. Veneers are strong but not indestructible.
  • Grinding: if you grind or clench, especially at night, a night guard protects veneers from cracking.
  • Very hard foods on front teeth: be sensible biting into hard items directly with veneered teeth.
  • Staining habits with composite: composite veneers can stain, so heavy coffee, tea, red wine and smoking take a toll over time.

Protecting against grinding

Grinding, known as bruxism, is one of the biggest threats to veneers, and many people do it in their sleep without knowing. If your dentist spots signs of grinding, a custom night guard is a small investment that protects a much larger one. It cushions the teeth at night and prevents the cracking and wear that grinding causes. If you wake with a sore jaw or your partner hears grinding, mention it.

Keeping them looking their best

Porcelain veneers resist staining well and tend to keep their colour for years. Composite veneers benefit from a little more care, and can be polished by your dentist to refresh them. Whatever the material, regular professional cleaning keeps the gum line healthy and the veneers looking their best. Avoid abrasive whitening products unless your dentist recommends them, since veneers do not whiten the way natural teeth do and harsh products can dull them.

Food and drink after veneers

You do not have to give up the things you enjoy, but a little awareness helps your veneers last. In the first day or two after fitting, while the bond fully settles, it is sensible to go easy on very hard or very hot and cold foods. After that, everyday eating is fine. The main thing to be mindful of long-term is habit rather than any single meal. Cutting hard foods like apples and crusty bread into pieces, rather than biting straight in with your front teeth, spares the edges of your veneers. If you drink a lot of staining drinks and have composite veneers, a straw and a rinse afterwards help keep them bright. None of this is about restriction. It is about small, easy habits that add years to how good your smile looks.

What to do if something feels wrong

If a veneer feels loose, rough, or chips, see a dentist rather than leaving it. Caught early, a small issue is usually a simple fix. Left alone, it can let bacteria under the veneer or lead to a lost restoration. For patients treated abroad, our remote follow-up can often tell you quickly whether something needs attention, and our UK partner network can arrange an in-person look if needed. Well cared for, veneers can serve you for many years, and the care they ask for is genuinely simple.
