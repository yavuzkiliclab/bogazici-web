Hidden costs to watch for, and how to avoid them

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

The price that draws you in is not always the price you pay. Some clinics advertise a low headline figure, then add extras once you are committed and far from home. Hidden costs are one of the most common complaints about treatment abroad, and almost all of them are avoidable if you know what to ask before you book. Here is where they hide and how to flush them out.

How hidden costs work

The pattern is familiar. A striking low price gets you through the door. Then, once you have travelled and treatment is under way, the additions appear: procedures that were "not included", materials upgraded without a clear choice, extra nights, extra fees. Each may sound reasonable in isolation. Together they can turn a bargain into something that costs more than treating at home, with far less room to walk away. The defence is to get everything in writing first.

The rule

If it is not in the written quote, treat it as a cost that could appear later. Ask until it is.

Common hidden costs to ask about

Extra procedures

Bone grafting, sinus lifts and extractions are sometimes genuinely needed, but they should be flagged as possibilities and priced up front, not sprung on you mid-treatment. Ask what happens, and what it costs, if your case turns out to need them.

Material upgrades

A low quote may assume budget materials, with better brands offered as a paid upgrade later. Ask which specific brands your quote is based on, so an "upgrade" is a real choice and not a surprise.

Accommodation and transfers

Check exactly how many nights are covered and whether every transfer is included. If healing means staying longer, know in advance who pays for the extra nights.

Temporary and follow-up work

Ask whether temporaries, adjustments and any second-stage work are in the price, or billed separately. These are easy to leave out of a headline figure.

The questions that expose them

You do not need to catch every trick. Two questions do most of the work. First: "Can I have a full, itemised quote in writing?" Second: "What could add to this price, and by how much?" A clinic that answers both clearly is one you can trust on cost. A clinic that gets vague is telling you where the extras will come from. Why written matters more than spoken

A friendly verbal reassurance costs nothing to give and nothing to break. A written, itemised quote is a commitment. When you ask for the price in writing, you are not being difficult, you are simply moving the promise from conversation to record. Good clinics expect this and provide it gladly. The ones that resist, or keep the figure vague and verbal, are the ones to be careful with. If a clinic is happy to put its price on paper and stand behind it, that single willingness tells you a great deal about how the rest of your treatment will be handled.
