Warning signs after treatment: when to seek help

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Some discomfort after dental treatment is normal. Some is not. Knowing the difference means you neither panic over nothing nor ignore something that needs attention. This is a plain guide to what is expected, what is worth a message, and what needs prompt help, so you can tell them apart with confidence.

What is normal in the first days

After many treatments, especially surgery like implants, some mild effects are expected and settle on their own. A degree of soreness around the site. Mild swelling for a day or two. Slight bruising. Tenderness when chewing nearby. Some sensitivity to hot and cold after fillings or crowns. These usually ease steadily over the first days to a week. Following the aftercare instructions you were given, prescribed medication, soft food, gentle cleaning, is what helps them settle.

The simple rule

Normal discomfort gets better each day. Anything getting worse, not better, is worth a message.

What is worth a message

Some things are probably fine but worth checking, so send a photo and a description and let the clinic judge.

  • Discomfort that is not easing after several days as expected.
  • A crown or filling that feels too high when you bite.
  • A rough edge, or something that catches your tongue.
  • Sensitivity that lingers longer than you were told to expect.

None of these are emergencies, but flagging them early is how small adjustments stay small.

What needs prompt attention

A few signs mean you should seek help without waiting. These are uncommon, but knowing them matters.

  • Pain that is getting worse rather than better after the first few days.
  • Swelling that is increasing, especially with fever or feeling unwell.
  • A restoration or implant component that has come loose or fallen out.
  • Bleeding that does not stop, or a bad taste with spreading swelling.

If any of these appear, contact us promptly. Where an in-person look is needed, our UK partner network can arrange for you to be seen at a partner treatment centre, and for anything urgent, local emergency dental care should be sought straight away.

Keep your instructions somewhere handy

The aftercare instructions you are given at the time are your best reference, because they are specific to what was done. Keep them somewhere you can find them in the first weeks, on your phone or pinned up at home. They tell you what is expected for your particular treatment, which medication to take and when, what to eat and avoid, and who to contact. When a question comes up at an odd hour, having the instructions to hand often answers it on the spot. If they do not, that is exactly when a photo and a message to the clinic earn their keep. The combination of clear instructions and an open line is what turns most worries into quick reassurance.

Trust the trend, not the moment

The single most useful principle is direction. Normal recovery improves day by day. If your discomfort is steadily easing, that is reassuring even if you are not fully comfortable yet. If it is going the other way, getting worse rather than better, that is the signal to reach out. When in doubt, a photo and a message cost you nothing and often save a lot of worry.
