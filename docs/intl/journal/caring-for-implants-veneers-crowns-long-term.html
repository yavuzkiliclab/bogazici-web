Caring for implants, veneers and crowns long-term

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Good dental work is an investment, and like any investment it lasts longer when you look after it. The good news is that caring for implants, veneers and crowns is not complicated. It is mostly the same habits that keep natural teeth healthy, with a few specifics worth knowing. Here is how to protect your new smile for the long run.

The foundation: daily habits

Almost everything comes down to two things done consistently: cleaning well and seeing a dentist regularly. Brush twice a day with a fluoride toothpaste, clean between your teeth daily, and keep your routine check-ups. This simple routine does more to protect your dental work than anything else. It is not glamorous, but it is what separates work that lasts decades from work that fails early.

The whole secret

Clean well, clean between the teeth, and keep your check-ups. Most dental work fails from neglect, not wear.

Caring for implants

An implant cannot decay, but the gum and bone around it can. This is the one risk that matters most, a condition called peri-implantitis, and it is driven by plaque and neglect, exactly like gum disease around natural teeth. Clean around your implants as carefully as anywhere else, use whatever interdental tools your dentist recommends, and do not skip check-ups, since early problems are easy to manage and late ones are not. Well cared for, an implant can last many years. You can read more in our implant guide and gum health guide.

Caring for veneers

Veneers are strong but not indestructible. Treat them like good teeth, not tools. Do not bite your nails, open packaging or crunch ice with them. If you grind your teeth at night, a night guard protects both veneers and natural teeth. Keep up normal cleaning, since the gum line where the veneer meets the tooth still needs care. Avoid habits that stain, and see your dentist if a veneer ever feels rough or loose.

Caring for crowns and bridges

Crowns and bridges need the same daily care as natural teeth, with extra attention to cleaning underneath and around a bridge, where food can gather. Your dentist can show you the right tool for cleaning under a bridge. The tooth under a crown can still decay at the margin, so cleaning that edge well matters. As with everything, regular check-ups catch small issues before they become big ones.

The tools worth having at home

A few simple tools make daily care easier and more effective. A soft-bristled toothbrush, or an electric one, cleans well without being harsh on the gum line. Interdental brushes or floss reach the spaces a brush misses, which is where problems around implants and bridges usually start. A water flosser can help around bridges and implants for people who find threading floss awkward. If you grind at night, a custom night guard is worth the investment to protect the work. None of this is expensive, and your dentist can point you to the right sizes and types for your mouth. The right tool used daily beats an elaborate routine done occasionally.

What shortens the life of dental work

A few things reliably cause trouble: poor cleaning, skipped check-ups, smoking, untreated grinding, and using teeth as tools. Avoid these and your work has every chance of lasting well. Most failures are not bad luck. They are the slow result of habits that could have been changed. The care is simple, and it is almost entirely in your hands.
