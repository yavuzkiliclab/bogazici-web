Crown materials compared: zirconia, porcelain and metal

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If you need a crown, the material it is made from affects how it looks, how strong it is, and how long it lasts. The main options are zirconia, porcelain fused to metal, and full metal, and each has its place. Understanding the differences helps you choose the right one with your dentist rather than simply accepting a default. This article compares them honestly.

Why material matters

Different crown materials balance appearance and strength differently. Some look strikingly natural but suit certain teeth better; others are extremely strong but less lifelike. The best material for a front tooth on show may not be the best for a back molar doing heavy chewing. A good dentist recommends the material that fits the specific tooth and your priorities, so knowing the options helps you understand their choice.

The quick guide

Zirconia: strong and natural, the modern all-rounder. Porcelain-metal: proven, slightly less lifelike. Metal: toughest, least natural.

Zirconia crowns

Zirconia has become a leading choice for good reasons. It is very strong and durable, yet can look natural, which makes it versatile across front and back teeth. Modern zirconia combines much of the strength of metal with an appearance close to natural tooth, avoiding the dark line that older crowns could show at the gum. For many patients it is the balanced modern option, strong enough for molars and natural enough for visible teeth.

Porcelain fused to metal

Porcelain fused to metal is a long-established, proven option: a metal base for strength with porcelain over it for a tooth-like appearance. It has decades of track record and works well in many situations. Its main limitation is that the metal underneath can sometimes show as a slight dark line at the gum over time, and it is a little less lifelike than all-ceramic options like zirconia. It remains a solid, reliable choice, particularly where cost matters.

Full metal crowns

Metal crowns, including gold alloys, are the strongest and most durable of all, and gentle on opposing teeth. Their obvious drawback is appearance: they look metallic, not natural. This makes them unsuitable for visible teeth but a sound choice for out-of-sight back molars where strength matters most and looks do not. They also require slightly less tooth removal than some alternatives. For a hidden molar under heavy load, metal is still a legitimate, long-lasting option.

What about allergies and sensitivity?

A practical factor that sometimes tips the choice is how your body responds to different materials. A small number of people are sensitive to certain metals, and for them all-ceramic options like zirconia have a clear advantage, since they contain no metal at all. Zirconia is also well tolerated by the gums and does not produce the faint dark line at the gum margin that a metal-based crown can develop over the years. If you know you react to particular metals, or you have had irritation from a previous restoration, tell your dentist before the material is chosen, as it genuinely narrows the sensible options. For most people this is not a concern and any of the materials is well tolerated, but it is the kind of detail worth raising early rather than discovering afterwards. A good dentist asks about allergies as a matter of routine when planning a crown, and factors your answer into the recommendation alongside the tooth position and your priorities.

How to choose

The right material depends on the tooth and your priorities. For a visible front tooth, appearance leads, favouring zirconia or all-ceramic. For a hidden molar under heavy chewing, strength leads, where zirconia or even metal makes sense. Cost, any allergies, and your bite also play a part. Discuss the specific tooth with your dentist and ask why they recommend a given material. A good clinic uses documented, quality materials whatever the type, as our certificate article notes.
