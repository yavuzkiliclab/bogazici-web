Can I have implants with gum disease?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If you have gum disease, you may wonder whether implants are even possible for you. It is an important question, because gum health and implant success are closely linked. The honest answer is that active gum disease needs treating first, but having had gum disease does not necessarily rule out implants. This article explains the relationship and what it means for you.

Why gum health matters for implants

Implants sit in the jawbone and gum, and they depend on healthy tissue around them to last. Active gum disease, which damages the gum and the bone that supports teeth, is the enemy of that stability. Placing an implant into an environment of active infection risks the same disease process affecting the implant, a condition that can lead to implant failure. This is why gum health is assessed carefully before any implant is planned.

The key sequence

Treat active gum disease first, then assess for implants. Having had it treated does not rule you out.

Active gum disease must be treated first

If you have active gum disease, it needs to be brought under control before implants are considered. This means treating the infection, cleaning below the gum line, and getting the gums healthy and stable. Only once the disease is controlled and the mouth is a healthy environment can implants be planned safely. Skipping this step is exactly the kind of corner-cutting that leads to problems, so a good dentist insists on it, as our what goes wrong article notes.

Having had gum disease is different

There is an important distinction between active gum disease and a history of it. Many people have had gum disease, had it successfully treated, and now have stable, healthy gums. For them, implants are often perfectly possible, provided the gums remain healthy and well maintained. What matters is the current state and ongoing control, not simply that you once had a problem. A history of gum disease means extra care and monitoring, not automatic exclusion.

The bone question

Gum disease that has been present for a while may have caused bone loss, since the disease destroys the bone supporting teeth. If enough bone has been lost, there may not be sufficient to hold an implant without rebuilding it first. Procedures like bone grafting can often restore enough bone to place an implant, as our bone grafting article explains. So past gum disease sometimes means an extra step, but frequently does not close the door.

What treating the gum disease involves first

If you do have active gum disease, understanding the step that comes before implants takes the uncertainty out of it. Treating gum disease usually starts with a deep clean below the gum line to remove the plaque and hardened deposits that drive the infection, sometimes over more than one visit. Your dentist will show you the daily cleaning technique that keeps the gums healthy afterwards, because the treatment only holds if the home care supports it. In more advanced cases, additional gum treatment may be needed to restore healthy tissue. Once this is done, there is usually a period of monitoring to confirm the gums have stabilised and stayed healthy before implants are planned. This might feel like a delay, but it is exactly what protects your future implants: placing them into a healthy, stable mouth is what gives them the best chance of lasting. A clinic that insists on this sequence is looking after your long-term result, not making things difficult.

The ongoing commitment

For anyone with a history of gum disease who has implants, the ongoing commitment is real and worth understanding. Excellent daily cleaning and regular professional maintenance are essential to keep the gums healthy and protect the implants long-term. Implants can develop their own version of gum disease if neglected, so maintenance is not optional. For patients treated abroad, our long-term care article and UK aftercare network support this ongoing care. With commitment, implants can succeed well even after gum disease.
