Composite vs porcelain veneers: which to choose?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If you have decided on veneers, the next choice is what they are made of. The two main options are composite and porcelain, and they are quite different in cost, durability and how they are done. Neither is simply better. The right one depends on your goals, your budget and your teeth. This article compares them honestly so you can choose with your dentist.

The two options in brief

Composite veneers are built up directly on your teeth from a tooth-coloured resin, shaped and polished by the dentist in the chair. Porcelain veneers are thin custom-made shells, crafted in a laboratory and then bonded to your teeth. That difference, built up directly versus made in a lab, drives everything else about them.

The short version

Composite is faster and cheaper. Porcelain lasts longer and looks more lifelike. Each suits different cases.

Composite veneers

Composite has real strengths. It is usually cheaper, can often be done in a single visit, and removes little or no tooth, which keeps it reversible in many cases. The trade-offs are that composite is less hard-wearing than porcelain, more prone to staining over time, and typically lasts a shorter number of years before needing refreshing. It suits smaller changes, tighter budgets, and those who want a quick, conservative improvement.

Porcelain veneers

Porcelain is the premium option for good reasons. It is harder-wearing, resists staining, and reflects light in a way that looks strikingly like natural tooth enamel, which is why it gives the most lifelike result. It lasts longer than composite, often many years. The trade-offs are a higher cost, the need for more than one stage since the veneers are lab-made, and a small amount of tooth preparation. It suits full smile makeovers and anyone wanting the most durable, natural-looking result.

How to choose

The decision comes down to a few honest questions. What is your budget? How long do you want them to last? How big a change are you making? For a small, reversible tweak on a modest budget, composite makes sense. For a full, long-lasting, natural-looking smile transformation, porcelain is usually worth the extra. Your dentist should lay out both, with the pros and cons for your specific case, rather than pushing one. You can read how many you might need in our how many veneers article.

What about maintenance?

Both materials ask for the same everyday care as natural teeth, but they age differently, and knowing that helps you choose. Composite can pick up stains over time from coffee, tea, red wine and smoking, much as natural teeth do, and it may need polishing or small repairs to keep it looking fresh. The upside is that composite is easy to repair or touch up in the chair. Porcelain resists staining far better and holds its appearance for longer, but if a porcelain veneer ever chips or fails, it is usually replaced rather than repaired. Neither is high-maintenance, but if you drink a lot of staining drinks or smoke, that is worth mentioning to your dentist, as it can tip the choice toward porcelain for a longer-lasting look.

The value view

It helps to think in terms of value, not just price. Composite costs less now but may need refreshing or replacing sooner. Porcelain costs more upfront but lasts longer and holds its appearance, which can make it better value across the years for a big makeover. Neither is a mistake when chosen for the right reasons. The mistake is choosing on headline price alone without weighing how long it lasts and how it will look. Get that balance right with your dentist, and either material can give you a smile you are happy with. Our aesthetics guide covers the wider picture.
