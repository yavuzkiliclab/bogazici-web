How long do dental implants last?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If you are going to invest in dental implants, it is fair to ask how long they will actually last. The honest answer is encouraging: implants are among the most durable treatments in dentistry, and a well-placed, well-cared-for implant can serve for decades. But the number depends on things you control more than things you do not. Here is what really determines the lifespan.

What the evidence shows

Implants are among the best-studied treatments in modern dentistry. Long-term research following implants for ten years or more reports survival rates well above ninety percent. That places them among the most reliable procedures a dentist can offer. The figure that matters for you, though, is not the average across thousands of patients. It is what happens with your implant, in your mouth, over your years, and that is shaped by care as much as by the implant itself.

The honest headline

A well-placed implant, well looked after, can last decades. Most failures come from neglect, not the implant.

The two parts, and their different lifespans

It helps to separate the implant from the tooth on top, because they last differently.

The implant itself

The titanium post fused into your jaw is designed to be permanent. Once it has integrated with the bone, it can remain stable for the rest of your life. It cannot decay, which removes the single biggest threat to a natural tooth.

The crown on top

The visible tooth, the crown, takes the daily wear of biting and chewing. Over many years it may need renewing, much like any restoration. Replacing a worn crown is straightforward and does not disturb the implant beneath it. So the foundation is for life, and the visible part is renewable.

What shortens an implant's life

The implant cannot decay, but the gum and bone around it can fall ill, a condition called peri-implantitis. It is driven by plaque and neglect, exactly like gum disease around natural teeth, and it is the main reason implants fail. Smoking is one of the strongest factors working against long-term success. Poor cleaning and skipped check-ups do the rest. Almost every one of these is within your control. You can read how to protect your implants in our long-term care article and our gum health guide.

How implant lifespan compares to the alternatives

Put next to the other ways of replacing a tooth, implants stand out for longevity. A bridge, which relies on the neighbouring teeth, typically needs replacing after a number of years, and each replacement involves those supporting teeth again. A denture is removable and tends to need adjusting and remaking over time as the mouth changes. An implant, by contrast, aims to be a permanent foundation that you clean and check like a natural tooth, with only the crown on top renewed if it wears. This is a large part of why implants, though a bigger investment at the outset, often work out well over a lifetime. You can weigh implant against bridge in our implant vs bridge article.

How to make yours last

The recipe is simple and boring, which is exactly why it works. Clean around your implants daily, including between the teeth. Keep your regular check-ups so any early problem is caught while it is small. Do not smoke. That is most of it. Do these, and your implant has every chance of lasting decades.
