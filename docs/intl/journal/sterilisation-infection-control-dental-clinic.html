Sterilisation and infection control: what to ask any clinic

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

It is one of the most sensible questions a patient can ask, and one of the least asked: how does the clinic keep its instruments clean? Infection control is invisible when it is done well, which is exactly why it pays to understand it. This article explains what proper sterilisation looks like in a dental clinic and the specific questions that get you a straight answer.

Why infection control matters more than it seems

Dentistry involves blood, saliva and open tissue. Every instrument that enters your mouth has to be either single-use or fully sterilised between patients. Done properly, the risk of cross-infection is extremely low. Done carelessly, it is the kind of risk that does not show up in a before-and-after photo. A clinic's sterilisation routine is one of the clearest signals of how seriously it takes the parts of care you cannot see.

A fair question

"How are your instruments sterilised between patients, and what do you use single-use?" A good clinic answers without hesitation.

What proper sterilisation looks like

Autoclaving

Reusable metal instruments are cleaned, then sterilised in an autoclave, a machine that uses pressurised steam at high temperature to kill all microorganisms. This is the standard across modern dentistry worldwide. Instruments come out sealed in pouches that are only opened at the chair, in front of you.

Single-use items

Anything that cannot be reliably sterilised, or that is cheaper and safer to discard, should be single-use: needles, certain tips, gloves, bibs and covers. These are opened fresh for each patient and thrown away afterwards.

Traceability

Well-run clinics track their sterilisation cycles, so each batch of instruments can be traced to a verified cycle. It is the same documentation habit that separates serious clinics in every other area, from implant certificates to treatment plans.

What single-use actually covers

It helps to know which items should never be reused, so you can notice if something looks off. Needles and anaesthetic cartridges are always single-use. So are the small suction tips, the plastic covers on lights and handles, gloves, masks and patient bibs. Burs and some instruments that touch bone or tissue are either single-use or sterilised to a high standard. If you ever see an item that looks reused when it should not be, that is a fair thing to raise. In practice, a well-run clinic makes a visible point of opening fresh, sealed items in front of you, precisely because it removes any doubt.

Questions that get you a straight answer

You do not need to be an expert to check this. Ask directly: are instruments autoclaved between patients? Which items are single-use? Are sterilisation pouches opened in front of me? A clinic proud of its standards will answer plainly and may even show you. Hesitation or vagueness is the answer to your question.

How this fits the bigger picture

Sterilisation sits alongside the other markers of a serious clinic: a facility licence, named dentists, documented materials and real aftercare. None of these is glamorous, but together they describe a clinic that does the invisible work properly.
