Sensitive teeth: causes and treatments

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

That sharp twinge when you eat something cold, hot or sweet is one of the most common dental complaints. Sensitive teeth are usually manageable once you understand the cause, and the fix is often simpler than people expect. This article explains what causes tooth sensitivity and what genuinely helps, so you can deal with it rather than just enduring it.

What causes sensitive teeth

Tooth sensitivity happens when the protective outer layers of a tooth are worn or breached, exposing the more sensitive inner layer to hot, cold and sweet stimuli. The common causes include enamel worn by aggressive brushing or acidic foods, gum recession exposing the sensitive root, tooth decay, a cracked tooth, worn fillings, or grinding. Sometimes it follows whitening or dental work and settles on its own. Identifying which cause applies to you is the key to the right fix.

The reassurance

Most sensitivity has a clear cause and a straightforward fix. It rarely means anything alarming.

The simple things that often help

For everyday sensitivity, some simple measures often make a real difference. Sensitivity toothpaste, used regularly, can genuinely reduce it over a few weeks by helping to block the pathways that carry the sensation. Switching to a soft toothbrush and brushing gently protects enamel and gums. Easing off very acidic foods and drinks helps, as does not brushing straight after acidic things. These low-cost steps resolve a lot of mild sensitivity without any treatment.

When it needs a dentist

Some sensitivity points to something that needs treating. Sensitivity that is severe, sudden, focused on one tooth, or comes with pain that lingers can indicate decay, a crack, a failing filling or a gum problem, and these need a dentist rather than just toothpaste. If sensitivity does not improve with simple measures, or gets worse, get it checked. Treating the underlying cause, whether a filling, addressing gum recession, or something else, is what resolves it properly.

The link with gums and grinding

Two common hidden causes are worth knowing. Gum recession, where gums pull back and expose the sensitive root, is a frequent cause and can relate to gum disease or overly hard brushing, as our gum health guide covers. Grinding, often at night, wears teeth and causes sensitivity, and a night guard can help. If your sensitivity is widespread or ongoing, these are worth exploring with your dentist, since addressing them tackles the root of the problem rather than the symptom.

Sensitivity after dental treatment

It helps to know that some sensitivity after dental work is normal and not a cause for alarm. After a filling, a whitening session, a deep clean, or having a crown fitted, teeth can feel more reactive to hot and cold for a short while as they settle. This kind of sensitivity typically eases over days to a couple of weeks and does not need treatment beyond patience and perhaps a sensitivity toothpaste. Whitening in particular commonly causes brief sensitivity, which is one reason having it done under a dentist's supervision helps, since they can manage it. What is not normal is sensitivity after treatment that is severe, gets worse rather than better, or comes with lingering pain when you bite, as that can mean a filling is too high, a bite needs adjusting, or there is an issue with the tooth underneath. In those cases, go back to your dentist for a check rather than waiting it out. For patients treated abroad, a quick photo and description through remote follow-up can usually tell you whether what you are feeling is ordinary settling or something that needs a proper look, which saves a lot of worry.

The bottom line

Sensitive teeth are common and usually very manageable. Start with the simple measures, sensitivity toothpaste, gentle brushing with a soft brush, and easing off acidic foods, which resolve a lot of mild cases. If sensitivity is severe, focused on one tooth, or does not improve, see a dentist to find and treat the cause. Rarely is it something alarming, but it is your teeth telling you something, so it is best not to simply ignore persistent sensitivity.
