Root canal and crown: why they often go together

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Patients are often surprised to be told they need a crown after a root canal. The two treatments go together far more often than people expect, and understanding why makes the recommendation clear rather than feeling like an add-on. This article explains the relationship between root canal treatment and crowns, so you know what to expect and why.

What a root canal does

A root canal treats infection or damage inside a tooth by removing the soft inner pulp, cleaning the space, and sealing it. It saves a tooth that would otherwise be lost to infection, and relieves the pain that comes with it. It is a genuinely tooth-saving treatment. But it changes the tooth in one important way that leads directly to the crown question.

The reason

A root-treated tooth is more brittle. A crown protects it from cracking, which is why the two so often go together.

Why a crown often follows

A tooth that has had a root canal is more brittle than a living tooth. Removing the pulp and, often, a good deal of the tooth structure to reach it, leaves the tooth weaker and more prone to cracking under the force of chewing. A crown caps and protects it, holding it together and letting it withstand normal use. This is why, particularly for back teeth doing heavy chewing, a crown after a root canal is standard advice rather than an upsell.

Is a crown always needed after a root canal?

Not always, and an honest dentist will say so. For some teeth, especially front teeth that do less heavy chewing and where less structure was removed, a filling may be enough. For back teeth under heavy load, or where a lot of the tooth is gone, a crown is usually the safe choice to prevent the tooth cracking and being lost. The decision depends on which tooth it is and how much structure remains, and a good dentist explains their reasoning for your specific tooth.

The cost of skipping it

When a crown is genuinely recommended after a root canal, skipping it is a false economy. A root-treated back tooth left without a crown can crack under chewing, and a cracked tooth may then be lost entirely, wasting the root canal that saved it. So while it feels like an extra step, the crown is often what protects the investment of the root canal. If a crown is advised, understanding this makes the recommendation easier to accept.

What to ask

If you are told you need a crown after a root canal, reasonable questions are: does this specific tooth need a crown, or would a filling do? Why? And what material do you recommend? A good dentist welcomes these and explains clearly. The goal is to protect a tooth that has just been saved, not to add treatment for its own sake.
