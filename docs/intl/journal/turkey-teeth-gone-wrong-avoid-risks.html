Turkey teeth gone wrong: the real risks, and how to avoid them

Published 17 July 2026· 7 min read

The phrase "Turkey teeth gone wrong" has fuelled a lot of headlines. Behind the scare stories there are real risks, but there is also a lot of missing context. This article covers what actually goes wrong, why it happens, and the concrete steps that prevent it. The goal is not to frighten you or reassure you. It is to help you avoid the mistakes that lead to bad outcomes.

What "gone wrong" usually means

Most bad outcomes fall into a few categories. Over-aggressive tooth preparation, where healthy teeth are filed down too far for crowns that were sold as veneers. Poor planning, where implants are placed without proper imaging or bone assessment. And no aftercare, where a patient is left with a problem and no one to turn to once they are home. Almost none of these are caused by the country. They are caused by the wrong clinic making the wrong choices.

The pattern

Most horror stories share a root cause: a rushed plan, an over-aggressive treatment, and no aftercare when it mattered.

The most common problems, and why they happen

Crowns sold as veneers

A true veneer removes only a thin layer from the front of a tooth. A crown removes far more. Some clinics fit crowns but call them veneers, filing down healthy teeth unnecessarily. Ask exactly what is being removed and why. You can read the real difference in our veneers guide.

Implants placed without proper planning

An implant needs a 3D scan, a bone assessment and a plan before anything is placed. Skipping these steps to save time is where implant failures begin. A good clinic will not place an implant it has not planned properly.

Too much treatment, too fast

A full mouth of crowns in a few days sounds efficient. It can also mean irreversible work done without time to think. Speed is fine when the plan is sound. It is dangerous when it replaces proper assessment.

No aftercare

The worst stories almost always include the same line: "and then I couldn't get hold of anyone." A problem that could have been a small fix becomes a big one because there was no route back to help.

What to do if it has already gone wrong

If you are reading this after a treatment that did not go to plan, the first step is a calm, professional assessment rather than panic. Get seen by a dentist who can examine what was done and document it, with X-rays if needed. Gather all your paperwork: your treatment plan, any implant certificates, and photos before and after. This record matters if you want the original clinic to put things right, or if another dentist takes over. Many problems that look alarming are fixable when handled properly, so an informed second opinion is worth more than a rushed reaction. If your treatment was with us and you are back in the UK, a review can be arranged in person through our partner network.

How to avoid it

Every one of these risks is avoidable with the right clinic. Insist on a written plan before you travel. Ask for a 3D scan and bone assessment for implants. Question anything irreversible. Confirm exactly what materials are used. And above all, ask what happens if you need help after you go home. With our UK partner network, that answer is a real one: in-person review at one of eight UK locations, not just an email address.
