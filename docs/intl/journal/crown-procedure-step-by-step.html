The crown procedure: what to expect step by step

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

If you have been told you need a crown, knowing exactly what the procedure involves takes away most of the worry. It is a routine, well-established treatment, and the steps are straightforward. This article walks through the crown procedure from start to finish, so you arrive knowing what happens and why, rather than wondering.

Step 1: Assessment and planning

It begins with an examination of the tooth and, usually, an X-ray to check the root and surrounding bone. This confirms that a crown is the right choice and that the tooth has a sound foundation to build on. Your dentist discusses the material options and what to expect, as our crown materials article covers. Good planning here is what makes the rest of the process smooth, so it is time well spent.

The shape of it

Prepare the tooth, take an impression, fit the crown. Straightforward, routine, and usually pain-free under anaesthetic.

Step 2: Preparing the tooth

With the area numbed by local anaesthetic, the dentist shapes the tooth to receive the crown, removing enough structure to make room for the cap to fit over it. If the tooth is broken down, a core may be built up first to give the crown something solid to sit on. This step is done under anaesthetic, so you should feel pressure but not pain. It is the stage that gives the crown a precise, well-fitting foundation.

Step 3: The impression

Next, a precise impression or digital scan of the prepared tooth is taken, so the crown can be made to fit exactly and meet your bite correctly. This is where a clinic with its own laboratory has an advantage, crafting the crown in-house to a precise fit. The colour is matched to your surrounding teeth for a natural look. Accuracy here is what makes the finished crown feel like your own tooth rather than a foreign object.

Step 4: Temporary and final crown

While your permanent crown is made, a temporary one usually protects the prepared tooth so you can eat and smile normally. On a treatment trip, a clinic with an in-house laboratory can often shorten the wait between preparation and fitting. When the permanent crown is ready, it is checked for fit, bite and appearance, adjusted if needed, and then bonded firmly in place. You leave with a restored tooth that looks and works naturally.

Is it painful?

For most people, having a crown fitted is not painful. The preparation is done under local anaesthetic, so you feel pressure and movement rather than pain. Some sensitivity afterwards, as the anaesthetic wears off and in the following days, is normal and settles. If the tooth needed a root canal first, that is a separate step, as our root canal and crown article explains. Overall, a crown is one of the more routine and comfortable restorative treatments.

Afterwards

Once your crown is fitted, it needs the same care as a natural tooth: good brushing, cleaning around the gum line, and regular check-ups. Mild sensitivity in the first days is normal. If your bite feels high or something is not right, tell your dentist so it can be adjusted, since a small tweak early keeps everything comfortable. With good care, a quality crown lasts many years, as our caring for crowns article covers.
