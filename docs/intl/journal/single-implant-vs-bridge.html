Single implant vs bridge: which is right?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

When you lose a single tooth, there are two main ways to fill the gap: a dental implant, or a bridge. Both work, both look natural, but they are quite different underneath, and the right choice depends on your situation. This article compares them honestly, so you can have an informed conversation with your dentist rather than being sold one option.

The key difference in one sentence

An implant replaces the missing tooth from the root up, standing on its own. A bridge fills the gap by using the two neighbouring teeth as anchors to carry a false tooth between them. That single difference, whether the neighbouring teeth are involved, drives most of the pros and cons.

The heart of it

An implant stands alone. A bridge leans on the teeth either side. That changes everything else.

The single implant

An implant is placed into the jaw where the tooth was, and a crown is fitted on top. Its great advantage is that it leaves the neighbouring teeth completely untouched. It also preserves the bone in the gap, which tends to shrink when a tooth is missing. It is a standalone solution that most closely mimics a natural tooth. The trade-off is that it involves minor surgery and a healing period, so it takes longer overall.

The bridge

A bridge is fixed to the teeth on either side of the gap, which are prepared and crowned to act as supports. Its advantages are speed and no surgery: it can often be completed faster and suits people who are not candidates for an implant. The trade-off is significant, though. It requires filing down two healthy neighbouring teeth to carry it, and it does not preserve the bone in the gap the way an implant does.

How to choose

Neither is universally better. The implant is often the preferred long-term choice when the neighbouring teeth are healthy, because it protects them and the bone. A bridge can be the better option when those neighbouring teeth already need crowns anyway, or when an implant is not suitable for reasons of bone or health. Your dentist weighs the condition of the neighbouring teeth, your bone, your general health and your priorities. What about cost and time?

Cost and time often tip the decision, so be clear-eyed about both. A bridge is usually quicker and can be less expensive at the outset, since it avoids surgery and a healing period. An implant costs more upfront and takes longer, because of the surgery and the months of fusing with the bone. But the fuller picture matters. A bridge that needs replacing over the years, sometimes more than once, can cost more across a lifetime than a single implant that lasts decades. So the cheaper option today is not always the cheaper option overall. Weigh the immediate cost against the likely lifespan, and factor in that an implant protects the neighbouring teeth from being drilled at all.

The long view

Think beyond the next few weeks. A bridge is quicker now but commits two healthy teeth to the job and may need replacing over time, sometimes involving those same teeth again. An implant asks for more time and a small surgery now, but stands alone and protects what is around it. For a healthy mouth with sound neighbouring teeth, that long view often favours the implant. For every case, the honest answer comes from your dentist after seeing your mouth, not from a rule of thumb.
