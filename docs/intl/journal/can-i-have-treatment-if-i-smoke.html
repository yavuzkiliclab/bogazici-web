Can I have treatment if I smoke?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Smokers considering dental treatment, especially implants, often ask whether they can have it at all. It is a fair question, because smoking does affect dental treatment and healing. The honest answer is that smoking does not usually rule out treatment, but it does raise the risks and matters more than many people realise. This article gives you the straight facts.

How smoking affects dental treatment

Smoking affects the mouth in ways that matter for treatment. It reduces blood flow to the gums, slows healing, and increases the risk of infection and gum disease. For implants in particular, which rely on healing and bone integration, smoking raises the risk of complications and implant failure compared with non-smokers. This is not a scare tactic, it is a well-established medical fact, and an honest dentist will tell you plainly.

The honest fact

Smoking rarely rules out treatment, but it raises the risks, especially for implants. Cutting down or stopping around treatment genuinely helps.

Does it rule out treatment?

For most treatments, smoking does not make you ineligible. Fillings, crowns, veneers and similar work can proceed, though good healing always helps. For implants, smoking is a risk factor rather than an outright barrier: many smokers have successful implants, but the risk of problems is higher, and a responsible dentist discusses this honestly with you before you decide. You are not automatically excluded, but you should go in with clear eyes about the raised risk.

Why stopping around treatment helps so much

The single most useful thing a smoker can do is reduce or stop smoking around the time of treatment, particularly surgery like implants. Even stopping for a period before and after the procedure meaningfully improves healing and lowers the risk of complications. It does not have to be forever to help, though quitting altogether is better for your oral and general health. A good clinic will encourage this and explain the timings, because it genuinely affects your result.

Being honest with your dentist

Whatever you do, be honest with your dentist about smoking. They need an accurate picture to plan safely, advise you properly, and set realistic expectations. Hiding it helps no one and risks your result. A good dentist responds to honesty with practical support, not judgement, helping you give your treatment the best chance. This openness is part of the same honest relationship that makes any treatment safer, as our candidate article reflects.

What about vaping?

A question that comes up more and more is whether vaping carries the same risks as smoking for dental treatment. The honest answer is that it is less studied than cigarettes, but it is not risk-free. Nicotine itself, whether from cigarettes or a vape, reduces blood flow to the gums, and that reduced blood flow is one of the main ways smoking impairs healing around implants. So while vaping avoids some of the harmful products of burning tobacco, the nicotine it delivers can still affect healing. The practical advice is much the same as for smoking: tell your dentist honestly if you vape, and consider reducing or pausing nicotine around the time of surgery to give healing the best chance. Do not assume that switching from cigarettes to a vape removes the concern entirely for implant healing. As with smoking, this is not usually a barrier to treatment, but it is a factor worth being open about so your dentist can plan properly and set realistic expectations.

The bottom line

Smoking rarely closes the door to dental treatment, but it raises the stakes, especially for implants, and it is something to take seriously rather than brush aside. If you smoke, you can very likely still be treated, but reducing or stopping around treatment, and being honest with your dentist, give you a real advantage. Treat it as one important factor in planning, discussed openly, and you and your dentist can make the right decision for your situation.
