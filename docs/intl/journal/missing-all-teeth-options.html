Missing all your teeth: what are the options?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

Losing all your teeth, or facing that prospect, is a difficult place to be, but the options for replacing them are better than many people realise. You are not limited to traditional dentures. This article lays out the main ways to replace a full arch or a full mouth of teeth honestly, so you understand the choices and can find the one that suits you.

The main options

There are three broad routes to replacing all the teeth in an arch: traditional removable dentures, implant-supported removable dentures, and fixed implant-supported teeth such as a full-arch bridge. They differ in stability, cost, and how they feel, and each suits different people and budgets. Understanding the differences is the first step to choosing well rather than defaulting to the first option offered.

The spectrum

From removable dentures, to dentures anchored by implants, to fixed teeth on implants. More stability costs more.

Traditional dentures

Removable dentures are the long-established option: a full set of replacement teeth that sits over the gums and can be taken out. Their advantages are that they are the most affordable route and involve no surgery. The drawbacks are stability and feel: they can move, affect what you can comfortably eat, and some people never fully get on with them. For many they work acceptably, but the limitations are real, and they are why implant options exist.

Implant-supported dentures

A step up in stability, implant-supported dentures clip onto a small number of implants placed in the jaw. The denture is still removable for cleaning, but it is held firmly, so it does not move or slip the way a traditional denture can. This gives much greater confidence eating and speaking, at a lower cost than fixed teeth, since it needs fewer implants. For many people it is a sweet spot between cost and stability.

Fixed implant-supported teeth

The most stable option is a full arch of fixed teeth supported by implants, sometimes provided as a full-arch bridge on several implants. These do not come out, they function much like natural teeth, and they feel the most secure. This is the premium route in both result and cost, and it is what many people mean when they picture a permanent solution. Our full-arch implants article covers this approach in detail.

Why replacing all your teeth matters

It helps to step back to why this decision deserves care, beyond simply filling gaps. Teeth do more than let you smile: they let you eat a full range of foods, which supports good nutrition, and they help you speak clearly. When all the teeth are missing and only a loose denture replaces them, some people quietly narrow their diet to soft foods and avoid eating in company, which affects both health and confidence over time. The jawbone also gradually shrinks once teeth are gone, which can change the shape of the face and make dentures fit less well as the years pass. Implant-based options slow this bone loss because the implants stimulate the bone much as natural tooth roots did. This is part of why, for someone facing full tooth loss, investing in a more stable solution is not just about appearance. It is about keeping the ability to eat well, speak confidently and maintain facial structure for years to come. An honest dentist frames the choice in these terms, not just as a price list, so you can weigh what each option means for your daily life.

How to choose

The right option balances stability, cost and your own preferences. If budget is the priority and surgery is unwanted, traditional dentures may suit. If you want firm, confident teeth without the top cost, implant-supported dentures are a strong middle path. If you want the closest thing to natural fixed teeth and can invest in it, fixed implant-supported teeth deliver that. An honest dentist lays out all three for your situation rather than pushing one, as our candidate article reflects. The best choice is the one that fits your life and your means.
