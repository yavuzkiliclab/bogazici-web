Am I a candidate for dental implants?

Published 17 July 2026· 6 min read

One of the most common worries people have is that they will not qualify for implants. They have heard you need enough bone, or that being older, or a smoker, or having a health condition rules them out. The reality is more generous than the myths. Most people are candidates, sometimes after a little preparation. This article explains what actually determines suitability.

The short answer

Most adults who have lost a tooth are potential candidates for an implant. The real question is rarely a flat yes or no. It is more often "yes, and here is what we would do first" if there is anything to address. Very few people are ruled out entirely. The only way to know your answer is an examination with 3D imaging, but the starting assumption for most people is that implants are possible.

The reassuring truth

Most people qualify. Where something needs sorting first, it usually can be. Outright "no" is rare.

What actually matters

Enough healthy bone

An implant needs bone to anchor into. Where bone has been lost, and it does shrink after a tooth goes missing, procedures like bone grafting or a sinus lift can rebuild the foundation first. So a lack of bone is usually a step to take, not a door closed.

Healthy gums

Gum disease must be brought under control before an implant is placed, because healthy gums are the foundation of a lasting implant. This is treatable, and once the gums are healthy, implant treatment can go ahead. Our gum health guide explains more.

General health

Most medical conditions do not rule out implants, but some need managing first. Well-controlled diabetes is usually not an obstacle, while uncontrolled diabetes can impair healing. Your dentist reviews your health and medications as part of planning.

The things people worry about needlessly

Several common fears are less limiting than people assume. There is no upper age limit for implants; health matters more than age, and many older patients do very well. Smoking works against success and is worth stopping, but it is a risk to manage rather than always an absolute barrier. A previous failed implant or old dental work rarely rules out trying again with proper planning. If you have talked yourself out of implants on one of these grounds, it may be worth asking rather than assuming.

When implants need groundwork first

Sometimes the answer is "yes, after some preparation". That might mean treating gum disease, rebuilding bone with a graft, or getting a health condition well controlled. This is normal and it is not a rejection. It is the clinic making sure the foundation is right so the implant lasts. You can read how implants are placed once you are ready in our implant guide, and how long they then last in our lifespan article.

How to find out for sure

The only real way to know is an assessment: a clinical examination and 3D imaging that show your bone, your gums and the specifics of your case. Everything before that is general guidance. If you have been wondering whether you qualify, the useful next step is simply to ask and be assessed, rather than deciding the answer for yourself.
