Is root canal painful, and can the tooth be saved?

Last updated · 9 July 2026 Medically reviewed · Dt. Edanur Alkan

The words "root canal" make many people think of pain. In truth, modern root canal treatment is not a procedure that causes pain but one that relieves it and saves the tooth. With modern anaesthesia and techniques, it is usually completed in comfort much like having a filling. This guide explains when a root canal is needed, how it is done, whether the tooth is saved and what to expect afterwards. Diagnosis and treatment are confirmed by your dentist after examination.

What root canal treatment is

Inside every tooth is a section called the pulp, holding the nerve and blood supply. This tissue nourishes the tooth as it develops; in a mature tooth, the tooth continues to be nourished through the surrounding tissues. When deep decay, a crack or trauma reaches the pulp, inflammation and infection can develop. Root canal treatment cleans this inflamed or infected tissue, shapes and fills the canals, and seals the tooth. The aim is to keep the tooth in place rather than remove it, which is why a root canal is an alternative to extraction and a way of saving the tooth.

When is a root canal needed?

The main signs that a root canal may be needed include:

  • Prolonged sensitivity to hot or cold that does not pass
  • Pain when chewing or on pressure
  • Spontaneous toothache, often worse at night
  • Swelling of the gum or an abscess near the tooth
  • Darkening of the tooth

In some cases the tooth is damaged silently with no marked pain; these are usually found on routine examination and X-ray. The presence or absence of symptoms alone does not confirm a diagnosis; assessment is made by the dentist.

Is a root canal painful?

This is the most asked and most misunderstood point. The bad reputation of root canal treatment really comes from the toothache that precedes it; the pain is caused by the inflammation that makes treatment necessary, not by the treatment. Because it is done under local anaesthesia, no pain is expected during the procedure. Most patients describe it as similar in comfort to having a filling. A few days of mild tenderness may follow, usually manageable with simple painkillers. In short, a root canal relieves pain rather than starting it.

How is a root canal done?

Treatment usually follows these steps:

  • 1. Anaesthesia: the area is numbed with local anaesthesia so no pain is felt.
  • 2. Access and cleaning: the inflamed tissue inside the tooth is reached and removed, and the canals are shaped.
  • 3. Filling: the cleaned canals are filled with a sealing material.
  • 4. Restoration: the tooth is closed with a filling or, often, a crown to restore function.

Depending on the tooth, treatment may take one or several sessions. Root-treated teeth, especially at the back, are often reinforced with a crown to withstand chewing forces.

Saving your own tooth

Wherever possible, keeping your natural tooth is preferred: it is a whole, in harmony with the bone and tissues around it.

Why a crown after a root canal?

A root-treated tooth can become more brittle than a living one, both because the inner tissue is removed and because the tooth has already lost structure to decay or a crack. In back teeth, where chewing forces are high, reinforcing the tooth with a crown after root canal treatment is often recommended. The crown wraps the tooth like a cap, reducing the risk of fracture and helping it last. In front teeth with little loss, a filling may be enough; the decision rests on how much sound structure remains and is made by the dentist. Completing the restoration in good time is important to the success of the treatment.

Root canal or extraction?

When a tooth is badly damaged, two paths come into view: saving it with a root canal, or extracting it and replacing it with an implant or bridge. The general approach is to keep your own tooth where possible, because a natural tooth is a whole, in harmony with the bone and tissues around it. But if the root cannot be saved, extraction and then filling the gap with an implant or bridge is considered. You can read about replacing a lost tooth in our implant guide.

Questions patients ask

How many visits does a root canal take?

Depending on the tooth and its canal anatomy, treatment can be completed in one or several sessions. The degree of infection and the tooth's position affect the time. Your personal plan is set after examination.

Does a root-treated tooth last a lifetime?

Properly done, suitably restored and well cared for, root-treated teeth can serve for many years. The main factors are the quality of the final restoration and regular oral care.

Will the tooth darken after a root canal?

In some cases a root-treated tooth may discolour over time. This can be corrected with aesthetic methods such as internal whitening or a veneer. If you notice discolouration, discuss it with your dentist.

Should I just have the tooth pulled instead?

The general approach is to keep your own tooth where possible, as a natural tooth is a whole with the surrounding tissues. If the tooth cannot be saved, extraction and an implant are considered. The decision depends on the tooth's condition.

Is pain after a root canal normal?

Mild tenderness and discomfort on chewing in the first few days can be normal and is usually managed with simple painkillers. Severe or prolonged pain should be reported to your dentist.
