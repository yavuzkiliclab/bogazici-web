How is an implant placed, and who is it right for?

Last updated · 9 July 2026 Medically reviewed · Dt. Mustafa Onur İmamoğlu

A dental implant is an artificial root, placed in the jawbone, that carries a crown, bridge or full arch of teeth. It is the closest modern dentistry comes to replacing a natural tooth from the root up. This guide explains how an implant is actually placed, who it suits, how long the process takes and what to expect during healing, so you can arrive at your consultation already informed. Your own treatment plan is confirmed by your dentist after a clinical examination.

What a dental implant is

The implant itself is a small screw, most often made of titanium. Titanium has a rare property: it fuses with living bone, a process called osseointegration, first demonstrated by the Swedish researcher Per-Ingvar Brånemark and his team in the 1960s [3]. Once fused, the implant becomes a stable foundation. It is built up in three parts: the body that sits in the bone, the abutment that connects to it, and the crown that shows as the visible tooth. For patients with titanium sensitivity, ceramic zirconia implants may be an option; your dentist advises after examination.

Who implants are right for

Implants are considered for situations ranging from a single missing tooth to a completely toothless jaw:

  • A single missing tooth: one implant and crown fill the gap without touching the neighbouring teeth.
  • Several missing teeth: an implant-supported bridge can replace a row of teeth.
  • A full arch: when most or all teeth are lost, four or six implants can carry a fixed set of teeth (All-on-4 or All-on-6), a fixed alternative to removable dentures.

Suitability depends on bone volume and density, gum health, general health and medications. Where bone is insufficient, procedures such as bone grafting or sinus lifting can rebuild the foundation first, so an initial lack of bone rarely rules implants out on its own.

Who should approach with care

Some conditions call for caution or preparation before implant treatment. Uncontrolled diabetes can impair healing and fusion; well-managed diabetes is usually not an obstacle. Untreated gum disease must be brought under control before placement, since a healthy gum is the foundation of a lasting implant. Heavy smoking is one of the strongest factors working against success [4]. Most of these are manageable risks rather than absolute barriers; your dentist weighs them together with your medical history.

How an implant is placed, step by step

A typical implant journey unfolds over a few stages:

  • 1. Examination and imaging: a clinical examination and 3D tomography map the bone. At our clinic, impressions are taken with an intraoral scanner rather than trays.
  • 2. Planning: the surgical plan is prepared on volumetric imaging, with the position, angle and size of the implant decided in advance.
  • 3. Placement: the implant is placed under local anaesthesia, with sedation available for anxious patients.
  • 4. Healing: osseointegration usually takes two to six months, varying by patient and site.
  • 5. Crown: once fused, the abutment is fitted and the permanent crown or bridge is made and placed, milled at our own bench.

How long does it take?

The surgery itself is usually a single session, but the total timeline is shaped by the healing period, most often two to six months. There is therefore no single answer to "how fast is it done": placement is quick, while the tooth settling into the bone takes months. For patients travelling to Istanbul, this is typically planned over two visits, a first for examination and placement and a second, after healing, to fit the permanent teeth. Your coordinator plans both trips with you.

Success rate and what affects it

Implants are among the best-documented treatments in dentistry. A systematic review of 7,711 implants followed for at least ten years reported a mean survival of 94.6 percent [1]; another meta-analysis put ten-year survival at 96.4 percent [2]. The factors that move these numbers are largely in your hands: daily hygiene, regular check-ups and not smoking. The implant itself cannot decay, but the gum and bone around it can fall ill, a condition called peri-implantitis, which is manageable when caught early [4]. Gum health is central to this, which is why we cover it in our gum health guide.

Healing and aftercare

The first days call for prescribed medication and soft food. Beyond that, an implant asks no more of you than a natural tooth: brushing twice a day, cleaning between teeth and regular check-ups [5]. Well cared for, an implant can serve for many years, while the crown on top may be renewed over time as it wears.

Questions patients ask

Is the procedure painful?

Placement is done under local anaesthesia and should not be painful during the procedure. The first days may bring mild swelling and tenderness, usually well managed with prescribed medication. Anxious patients can choose sedation.

I was told I lack bone. Is it still possible?

Often, yes. Bone grafting and sinus lifting can rebuild the foundation before or during placement. Your tomography shows exactly what is possible, and your dentist will walk you through your own scan before anything is decided.

Can I have implants with diabetes?

Patients with well-controlled diabetes can usually have implant treatment. What matters is not the presence of diabetes but how well it is managed, since uncontrolled blood sugar can impair healing. Your general health is assessed before treatment.

Is there an age limit for implants?

Implants can be considered for any adult whose jaw has finished growing; there is no upper age limit. What decides suitability is bone structure and general health, not age. In younger patients still growing, implants are deferred.

What is All-on-4?

All-on-4 is a full arch of fixed teeth carried on four implants; All-on-6 uses six. Both replace removable dentures with teeth that stay in place. Which suits you is decided by bone volume and bite during planning.
